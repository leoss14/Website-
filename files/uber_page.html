Geographic Evolution of Uber Demand in NYC

Between January 2018 and January 2025, Uber's market share of for-hire vehicle trips in New York City grew from 23% to 75%. This project documents how the spatial distribution of pickups and dropoffs shifted over that period, using NYC Taxi and Limousine Commission trip records, and tests whether observed changes are consistent with the deployment of value-based dispatch algorithms.

Background

Concepts & Research Design

Greedy matching is the dispatch rule Uber used in its earlier years: assign the closest available driver to each passenger request. This minimizes immediate pickup time but ignores where the driver ends up after the trip. A driver might pick up a rider two minutes away in Midtown and drop them in an isolated area, earning well on that single ride but then facing a long wait for the next one. Meanwhile, a more distant driver could have taken the same trip and ended up better positioned for subsequent pickups.

MDP-based matching (Markov Decision Process) is the alternative approach described in Xu et al. (2018). Instead of minimizing pickup distance, it maximizes long-run system value. The system learns a value for each geographic zone at each time of day, based on millions of historical observations. Each dispatch decision evaluates: Immediate Fare + Future Position Value − Current Position Value. Drivers in low-value zones get priority for trips to high-value destinations; trips to high-demand areas are valued because they set up the next ride.

Spatial autocorrelation, measured by Moran's I, tests whether high-demand zones tend to cluster together geographically (positive autocorrelation) or are spread more evenly across the city (lower autocorrelation). The Gini coefficient measures overall concentration of pickups across zones regardless of their spatial arrangement. LISA (Local Indicators of Spatial Association) identifies individual hot spots and cold spots at the zone level: zones with statistically significant high demand surrounded by other high-demand neighbours, and vice versa.

Research Question

How did the spatial distribution of Uber pickups in NYC change between 2018 and 2025, and are the observed patterns consistent with value-based algorithmic dispatch?

The central finding is that demand redistributed in ways consistent with MDP-based matching: airport zones gained share, Manhattan core zones declined, spatial autocorrelation fell substantially, and trips became more localised. Temporal patterns (when people use the service) remained stable, suggesting that the geographic shifts reflect supply-side reallocation rather than changes in rider behaviour.

Analytical Approach

The analysis proceeds in three stages. First, geographic analysis compares the spatial distribution of Uber pickups across NYC's 263 TLC taxi zones, using K-means clustering, Gini coefficients, and Lorenz curves to measure concentration. Second, spatial autocorrelation analysis (global Moran's I and local LISA maps) tests whether high-demand zones cluster geographically and how that clustering changed. Third, temporal analysis examines whether hourly and daily demand patterns shifted between years, providing a control for demand-side explanations. The project generates six testable predictions from MDP theory and evaluates each against the data.

2018: Greedy Matching

Rule: Assign the closest available driver to minimize pickup time. Ignores post-trip driver positioning. Can leave drivers stranded in low-demand areas. Optimizes pickup time at the expense of system-wide throughput.

2025: MDP-Based Matching

Rule: Assign the driver whose dispatch maximizes long-run value. Drivers in low-value zones get priority for high-value destinations. Trips to high-demand areas are valued because they position drivers for subsequent rides.

Worked Example

A passenger in Midtown requests a trip to JFK Airport. Two drivers are available. Driver A is 2 minutes away, already in Midtown. Under greedy matching, Driver A gets the fare but then sits in the JFK queue for 30 minutes; Midtown loses a well-positioned driver. Driver B is 5 minutes away, on the Upper East Side. Under MDP matching, Driver B takes the airport fare because Driver B has less to lose by leaving a lower-value zone. Driver A stays in high-demand Midtown and picks up the next ride quickly. Both end up better off. Xu et al. (2018) report that MDP-based dispatch improved trip completion rates by 0.5% to 5% across 20+ cities.

Predictions

Testable Predictions from MDP Theory

If value-based matching was deployed between 2018 and 2025, specific changes in demand geography should follow. Each prediction is tied to a concrete mechanism in the dispatch model.

Spatial Autocorrelation

The Gini coefficient measures overall concentration but not spatial structure: it treats zones as interchangeable regardless of location. Moran's I tests whether high-demand zones tend to cluster together geographically, or whether demand is more evenly spread across the city. A higher value indicates stronger spatial clustering, meaning high-demand zones are surrounded by other high-demand zones and low-demand zones are surrounded by low-demand neighbours.

Between 2018 and 2025, global Moran's I dropped from 0.55 to 0.28 (both significant at p<0.001). This is a substantial decline: demand is still spatially structured, but far less polarised between high-activity and low-activity neighbourhoods. The gap between the core and the periphery narrowed, which is what a dispatch system that routes supply toward underserved areas would produce.

The LISA maps below show this at the zone level. Red zones are statistically significant hot spots (high demand surrounded by high demand). Blue zones are cold spots. In 2018, eastern Queens and much of the Bronx formed a large contiguous cold spot. By 2025, that block had shrunk: many outer-borough zones flipped from statistically significant cold spots to non-significant, meaning their demand levels became indistinguishable from their surroundings.

The most pronounced shift occurred between Manhattan's traditional high-density zones and the airports. JFK and LaGuardia combined increased their share of pickups by 48%, while the top Manhattan zones (Union Square, Midtown Center, East Village) declined by an average of 35%.

Cluster Movement

To quantify geographic shift, each 2018 cluster centroid was matched to its nearest 2025 counterpart by geographic proximity (not index order) and the displacement was measured. The map below shows how the six demand centres moved over the seven-year period.

Cluster Centroid Shifts: 2018 → 2025

Cluster centroids shifted 4.64 km on average. Most movement was directed toward airports and outer-borough transit hubs. The moderate scale of these shifts, relative to the full extent of NYC, indicates reallocation within an established spatial structure rather than wholesale reorganisation.

Intra-Cluster Trip Share

The share of total trips where the pickup and dropoff fall within the same geographic cluster increased from 63.2% in 2018 to 71.9% in 2025, suggesting that rides are completing closer to where they started. This is consistent with more efficient local matching, where drivers are dispatched to nearby riders rather than being sent across cluster boundaries.

Intra-Cluster Trip Share (2018 vs. 2025)

Caveat on Cluster Boundaries

The clusters are fit independently per year, so boundary differences may account for part of the intra-cluster share increase. However, the direction is consistent with the broader pattern of spatial dispersion documented above.

Borough-Level Distribution

Aggregating by borough provides a macro view of the redistribution and helps distinguish between internal reshuffling within Manhattan and net expansion into outer boroughs.

Pickup Share by Borough (2018 vs. 2025)

Manhattan retained roughly 68% of Uber pickups but with reduced internal concentration: fewer trips in the traditional core, more in upper Manhattan. Brooklyn's share grew from 18% to 20%. Queens held steady at around 9%. The outer-borough uptick represents expansion into areas that were less served in 2018, not a substitution away from Manhattan.

Part II

Temporal Demand Patterns

While geographic patterns shifted substantially, temporal patterns remained stable. The peak hour stayed at 6 PM in both years. The busiest day shifted from Saturday (2018) to Friday (2025), though the overall weekly shape changed only modestly. This matters for interpretation: if the geographic redistribution were driven by changes in rider behaviour (commuting patterns, remote work, post-pandemic shifts), temporal patterns should shift as well. Their stability is more consistent with supply-side reallocation.

Demand Heatmap: Hour × Day

The heatmaps below show the share of total pickups by hour and day of week for each year, with a third panel showing the difference. The temporal distribution is nearly identical across years: evening peaks on weekdays, more uniform demand on weekends. Synthesis

Interpretation

The geographic, temporal, and spatial autocorrelation results point to four main observations about the evolution of Uber demand in NYC.

01

Supply-side reallocation, not demand-side reorganisation

Temporal patterns (peak hours, daily distribution) remained stable between years, while geographic patterns shifted substantially. If changes in rider behaviour (commuting, remote work, post-pandemic shifts) were the primary driver, both dimensions should have moved. The stability of the temporal dimension points toward supply-side mechanisms.

02

Spatial dispersion within a stable structure

The Moran's I decline (0.55 → 0.28) is the strongest individual finding. Previously underserved outer-borough zones gained enough activity to become statistically indistinguishable from their surroundings. The 4.64 km average cluster shift indicates these changes occurred within the existing geographic structure of NYC demand, not through fundamental reorganisation.

03

Airport zones replaced Manhattan core as the top demand centres

JFK displaced Midtown Center as the top pickup zone. Combined airport pickup share rose 48%, while top-3 Manhattan zones declined 34–39%. This is consistent with MDP valuation: airports position drivers for high-value return fares, making them attractive dispatch destinations for the algorithm.

04

Correlation, not causation

The seven-year gap between observations means any difference reflects everything that changed in that period: post-pandemic shifts, competitive dynamics between Uber and Lyft, fare changes, the 2019 TLC cap on new vehicle licenses, airport ground transportation policy, and shifts in where New Yorkers live and work. A controlled experiment or regression discontinuity around a known algorithm deployment date would be needed to establish causality.

Limitations

Causal Identification

This analysis documents correlation, not causation. The seven-year gap means any observed difference reflects all intervening changes: pandemic shifts, competition, fare changes, regulatory interventions (the 2019 TLC vehicle cap), and changes in where New Yorkers live and work.

Temporal Coverage

The data covers January only, so seasonal variation is not captured. Results may look different in summer months or during holiday periods.

Cluster Independence

K-means clusters are fit independently per year, so boundary differences may partly account for the intra-cluster share increase. Zone-level mismatch ratios showed no significant change in variance (Levene's p=0.95), indicating that per-zone supply-demand balance was already high in 2018 and remained so.

Alternative Explanations

Post-pandemic commuting behaviour, Uber-Lyft competitive dynamics, airport ground transportation policy changes, and Uber's own pricing adjustments are all plausible contributors to the observed patterns and cannot be ruled out.

Methodological Notes

Data

NYC TLC public trip records (Parquet format). January 2018: 19.8M total FHV trips (4.5M Uber). January 2025: 20.4M total FHV trips (15.4M Uber). 263 TLC taxi zones. Uber identified via HVFHS license number. Zone centroids from NYC Open Data shapefile.

Statistical Methods

K-means clustering (k=6) on zone centroid lat/lon. Clusters matched across years by geographic proximity. Gini coefficient and Lorenz curves for concentration. Global Moran's I and local LISA (KNN weights, k=6) for spatial autocorrelation. Kolmogorov-Smirnov test for distributional comparison. Levene's test for variance equality.

Reference: Xu et al. (2018). "Large-scale order dispatch in on-demand ride-hailing platforms." KDD '18. MDP framework based on published research. Analysis independent of any proprietary system.

Reinforcement Learning for Monetary Policy Optimisation

Training a DQN agent on 50 years of US macroeconomic data to set interest rates, benchmarked against the Taylor Rule and actual Federal Reserve decisions.

Timeline Feb 2026
Data 1974–2023
Frequency Monthly
DQN vs Taylor −27% MAD

Summary

A Deep Q-Network trained on a neural-network inflation forecast produces interest-rate recommendations that track actual Federal Reserve decisions more closely than the standard Taylor Rule across five decades of history. The DQN agent achieves a mean absolute deviation of 3.14 pp from the Fed, compared to 4.30 pp for the Taylor Rule — a 27% reduction.

The project tests five model specifications under expanding-window time-series cross-validation across distinct economic regimes. A focused 8-feature specification (LEAN) outperforms higher-dimensional alternatives, demonstrating that parsimonious models generalise better for 12-month-ahead inflation forecasting.

Motivation

Central banks set interest rates to steer inflation toward a target while stabilising employment and output. The Taylor Rule provides a transparent benchmark, but it is a fixed formula that cannot adapt to shifting economic dynamics. This project explores whether a reinforcement learning agent, trained on historical data, can learn a more flexible rate-setting policy.

The Taylor Rule

John Taylor's 1993 rule prescribes the nominal interest rate as a linear function of the inflation gap and the unemployment gap:

i = r* + π + 1.5(π − π*) + 0.5(u − u*)

Where r* = 2% (neutral real rate), π* = 2% (inflation target), and u* = 5% (natural unemployment rate). The rule is simple and historically tracks Fed decisions reasonably well during stable periods, but it tends to overshoot during recessions and structural breaks.

The RL Approach

Following Hinterlang & Tanzer (2021) and related literature on RL for macroeconomic policy, this project takes a two-stage approach:

1. A neural network learns to forecast inflation 12 months ahead using lagged macroeconomic indicators — the only learned transition in the environment.
2. A DQN agent sets monthly interest rates within that environment, optimising a weighted loss over inflation deviations, unemployment deviations, and rate-smoothing.

Unemployment and capacity utilisation evolve from historical data rather than from a second learned model. This avoids the compounding-error problem that arises when multiple transition models are chained together.

Methodology

Information Structure

Central banks face publication delays: economic data is typically available with a lag of 1–6 months. To test the effect of these constraints, we create lagged features at two horizons:

Realistic lags (18, 24, 30 months): approximate the information set available after publication delays plus a 12-month lookback.
Intermediate lags (3, 6, 12 months): provide more recent signal, improving forecast accuracy at the cost of realism.

The target variable is inflation 12 months ahead — the standard central-bank forecast horizon.

Model Specifications

Five specifications test how lag horizon, variable breadth, and dimensionality affect forecast accuracy:

Specification Features Lags Description
SIMPLE 6 18, 24, 30 Inflation + fed funds, realistic lags
EXPANDED 12 18, 24, 30 + unemployment gap, capacity gap
FULL 18 18, 24, 30 + term spread, financial conditions
INFORMATIVE 36 3–30 All variables, all lags
LEAN 8 3, 6, 12 Focused: inflation, rate, unemp gap, term spread

Inflation Forecast

MLP neural network (64-32 hidden units) predicts inflation 12 months ahead.

Architecture: 2-layer MLP, ReLU

Regularisation: α = 0.01

Validation: Time-series CV (5 folds)

RL Environment

State: [inflation, unemployment, capacity, rate]

Actions: 41 discrete rates (0–20%)

Episodes: 36 months (3 years)

Reward: −(π_dev² + 0.5·u_dev² + 0.1·Δi²)

DQN Agent

Network: 128-128 Q-network (PyTorch)

Target network: Updated every 400 steps

Replay buffer: 15,000 transitions

Training: 600 episodes (~21,600 steps)

Parsimony Outperforms Dimensionality

LEAN (8 features) outperforms INFORMATIVE (36 features) despite using far fewer variables. With ~460 training observations, the higher-dimensional specifications overfit. The focused feature set captures the core macro signal without excess model capacity.

Regime Sensitivity

All specifications struggle on the Early 1990s fold (trained on 1973–1990, tested on 1991–1995). Models trained on 1970s–80s volatility extrapolate poorly into the Great Moderation — a structural limitation of any lag-based forecast model across regime changes.

Policy Comparison

Taylor Rule 4.30 5.82 Baseline

The DQN agent tracks actual Fed decisions more closely than the Taylor Rule across most of the sample. The Taylor Rule's largest deviations occur during the Volcker disinflation (early 1980s), the zero-lower-bound period (2009–2015), and the post-COVID inflation surge (2021–2023) — episodes where a fixed-coefficient rule cannot adapt to structural shifts in the policy environment.

Limitations

Caveats

Forecast model accuracy: The inflation forecast has limited out-of-sample accuracy, particularly across regime changes. The agent's policy quality is bounded by the quality of the underlying model.

Partial equilibrium: Unemployment and capacity evolve from the historical record rather than responding to the agent's rate choices. A full general-equilibrium model would capture these feedback effects but introduces the compounding-error problem.

Rate anchoring: The agent's tendency to stay near the current fed funds rate partly reflects the rate-smoothing penalty in the reward function rather than learned macro dynamics.

Lucas critique: Learned policies assume the economy's response to interest rates remains stable — agents in the economy may change behaviour under a different policy regime.

Extensions

Calibrated New Keynesian environment with known IS/Phillips dynamics to isolate the RL contribution from forecast model quality. PPO or SAC algorithms for more stable training. Multi-country comparison (ECB, BoE, BoJ). Policy distillation to extract interpretable decision rules from the trained network.

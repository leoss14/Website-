Natural Resources & Economic Complexity

Econometric analysis of resource dependence and development pathways across 208 countries. Capstone project with Moody's Ratings.

Research Question

Do natural resource endowments constrain or enable economic diversification? How do resource-rich countries differ in their paths toward economic complexity?

The "resource curse" hypothesis suggests that countries rich in natural resources often experience slower economic growth and lower development outcomes compared to resource-poor countries. This project examines this relationship empirically across 208 countries, analyzing patterns of natural resource production alongside measures of economic complexity, human capital, and institutional quality.

Analytical Approach

The analysis integrates unsupervised learning (PCA and K-means clustering), econometric methods (panel regression with fixed effects), and supervised learning (regularized linear models and gradient-boosted trees across three specifications) to identify patterns in how countries navigate resource wealth. Production data for 20+ commodities are aggregated into four categories — oil, natural gas, coal, and metals — and normalized by GDP to measure resource intensity.

Key Findings

1. Resource Production is Highly Concentrated

Global natural resource production exhibits extreme concentration. A small number of countries account for the majority of production across all resource categories, while most countries show minimal resource intensity.

  • Top 3 producers dominate: China ($280B), USA ($273B), and Russia ($201B) account for the majority of global production
  • High resource dependence is rare: Only a handful of countries show resource production exceeding 10% of GDP (Kuwait 15.4%, Qatar 12.5%, Iraq 10.5%)
  • Hydrocarbon leaders: Top absolute oil producers include major exporters and diversified industrial economies
  • Metals concentration: Production concentrated in China, Australia, and Latin American mining economies

2. Two Principal Dimensions Structure Resource-Development Relationships

PCA reveals that variation in resource-economic patterns is primarily structured along two orthogonal dimensions:

PC1 - Economic Development (31.6%)

Countries vary primarily by economic sophistication and human capital, largely independent of resource endowments. High-complexity economies cluster together regardless of resource production.

PC2 - Hydrocarbon Intensity (24.1%)

A second dimension captures oil and gas production intensity, identifying petrostates as a distinct category. This operates independently of general development level.

This suggests that while economic development and resource wealth are related, they represent distinct sources of variation - economic complexity is not mechanically determined by resource abundance.

4. Human Capital Effects Are Diminished in Resource-Rich Contexts

The relationship between resources and complexity is mediated by human capital investment. However, the interaction effect reveals a constraint rather than an opportunity.

The HCI × Total Resources interaction term is negative (−0.274), indicating that returns to human capital are diminished in resource-rich contexts. This suggests that resource abundance may undermine the institutional or incentive structures that allow education to translate into economic diversification.

This finding supports resource curse mechanisms where natural wealth discourages the development of complex productive capabilities, even when human capital is present. It suggests that overcoming resource dependence requires not just education but structural reforms that create demand for skilled labor outside the extractive sector.

Cluster Visualizations

Cluster Transitions (1995–2019)

Static cluster assignments mask an important question: how stable are these groupings over time? The Sankey diagram below traces country movements between clusters from 1995 to 2019, revealing which development pathways are common and which are rare.

Transition Patterns

Of 126 countries tracked across both years, 100 countries (79%) remained in their original cluster. The remaining 26 countries (21%) shifted categories. Several patterns stand out:

  • Upward mobility is possible but uncommon: 12 countries moved from Low-Income Diversified to Advanced Economies — Brazil, Malaysia, Thailand, Türkiye, Philippines, South Africa, Portugal, Uruguay, Tunisia, Costa Rica, Cyprus, and Panama. These transitions typically accompanied sustained manufacturing development and human capital accumulation.
  • Mining intensification: 6 countries moved into Mining-Dependent status — Burkina Faso, Bolivia, DRC, Ghana, Mali, and Peru — reflecting expanded metals extraction relative to GDP.
  • Petrostate persistence: 10 of 13 petrostates in 1995 remained petrostates in 2019. Norway and Qatar moved to Wealthy Hydrocarbon Exporters, combining high oil/gas production with greater economic complexity.
  • No petrostate reached Advanced Economies: No country transitioned directly from Petrostates to Advanced Economies during this period, confirming the difficulty of escaping oil dependence.
  • Other notable transitions: Gabon moved from Petrostates to Mining-Dependent; Bahrain moved from Mining to Advanced Economies; Mongolia became the sole Coal & Metals Outlier; Uzbekistan moved from Wealthy Hydrocarbon Exporters to Low-Income Diversified.

Econometric & Machine Learning Results

Econometric Insights

  • Resource Curse: When controlling for country and year fixed effects, resources do not inherently reduce complexity
  • Human Capital: HCI consistently positive and significant
  • Interaction Effect: HCI × Total Resources term is negative (−0.274), indicating that returns to human capital are diminished in resource-rich contexts
  • Institutions Matter: Rule of law and property rights show positive associations

Machine Learning Insights

  • Top Predictors: Property rights (0.31) and human capital (0.17) dominate feature importance in the XGBoost model
  • Human Capital Constraint: The negative HCI × Total Resources interaction (−0.274) indicates that resource wealth undermines the translation of education into economic diversification
  • Model Performance: XGBoost achieves test R² = 0.816 with interactions; Random Forest reaches 0.891 with full structural controls
  • Interaction Value: Adding the HCI × Total Resources interaction improves test R² by 0.012 (1.2 percentage points) over baseline

Model Performance

Three model specifications are compared: RC Baseline (8 features: resource intensities, ECI, HCI), RC + Interactions (10 features: baseline + HCI × Total Resources + high-resource dummy), and Full Structural (17 features: interactions + landlocked, property rights, rule of law, trade openness, polity, population, GDP per capita). The interaction model tests whether the interaction between human capital and resource production affects complexity. Panel data uses a temporal split: training on observations through 2013, testing on 2014–2019. The sample includes 54 high-resource countries (resource rents >5% GDP in 1995) and 72 others.

Policy Implications

Context-Dependent Resource Curse

The resource curse is not universal. Oil production associates positively with complexity in diversified economies (Canada, Norway) but negatively in resource-dependent countries. Policy should focus on the conditions under which resources become constraints rather than treating resource wealth as inherently problematic.

Heterogeneous Strategies

The interaction analysis suggests differentiated approaches:

  • High-resource, low-complexity: Prioritize human capital and institutional reform
  • Diversified resource producers: Leverage resource revenues for complexity-enhancing investments
  • Metals-dependent economies: Focus on downstream processing and manufacturing linkages

Methodological Notes

Data Processing

  • Resource Aggregation: 20+ commodities grouped into 4 categories
  • Production Values: Physical quantity x market price (USD)
  • GDP Normalization: Production / (GDP per capita x Population)
  • Standardization: Mean=0, Std=1 before PCA/clustering

Statistical Methods

  • PCA: 2 components, 55.7% variance explained
  • K-means: k=6, silhouette score = 0.344
  • Panel Regression: Country and year fixed effects
  • ML: Temporal split (train ≤2013, test 2014-2019), ridge/tree models with interaction terms

Appendix: Methodological Details

Resource Diversity & Intensity Indices

Two complementary indices measure how countries relate to natural resources. Diversity uses Shannon entropy across five resource categories (fossil fuels, base metals, precious metals, battery/strategic metals, industrial minerals), counting only resources where the country holds at least 0.5% of global production. The entropy is normalized to [0, 1] by dividing by ln(5). A score near 1 indicates production spread evenly across all five categories; a score near 0 indicates concentration in a single category.

Intensity measures total resource production value as a share of GDP, capturing how much the domestic economy depends on extraction. The map uses a log scale because the range is wide — from near-zero for service economies to above 15% for countries like Kuwait and Congo Republic.

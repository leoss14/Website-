Natural Resources & Economic Complexity

Econometric Analysis of Resource Dependence and Development Pathways
Moody's Ratings Capstone Project · London School of Economics
Master in Public Administration - Data Science for Public Policy · 2025

Research Question

Do natural resource endowments constrain or enable economic diversification? How do resource-rich countries differ in their paths toward economic complexity?

The "resource curse" hypothesis suggests that countries rich in natural resources often experience slower economic growth and lower development outcomes compared to resource-poor countries. This project examines this relationship empirically across 208 countries, analyzing patterns of natural resource production alongside measures of economic complexity, human capital, and institutional quality.

Analytical Approach

The analysis integrates unsupervised learning (PCA and K-means clustering), econometric methods (panel regression with fixed effects), and machine learning (regularized regression) to identify patterns in how countries navigate resource wealth. Four resource categories are examined: hydrocarbons (oil, gas, coal), metals (iron, copper, gold, etc.), minerals (phosphate, potash), and agricultural commodities.

Key Findings

1. Resource Production is Highly Concentrated

Global natural resource production exhibits extreme concentration. A small number of countries account for the majority of production across all resource categories, while most countries show minimal resource intensity.

  • Top 3 producers dominate: China ($280.5B), USA ($273.2B), and Russia ($200.9B) account for 61.6% of production among the top 10 producers
  • High resource dependence is rare: Only 10 countries show resource production exceeding 7.5% of GDP (Kuwait 15.8%, Congo Rep. 15.1%, Qatar 12.5%)
  • Hydrocarbon concentration: USA, Saudi Arabia, and Russia dominate oil production ($176.5B, $121.9B, $120.3B respectively)
  • Metals concentration: China alone produces $90.7B in metals, followed by Australia ($25.6B) and Chile ($24.4B)
  • Resource dependence remains the exception globally, not the norm

2. Two Principal Dimensions Structure Resource-Development Relationships

PCA reveals that variation in resource-economic patterns is primarily structured along two orthogonal dimensions:

PC1 (Economic Development): Countries vary primarily by economic sophistication and human capital, largely independent of resource endowments. High-complexity economies cluster together regardless of resource production.

PC2 (Hydrocarbon Intensity): A second dimension captures oil and gas production intensity, identifying petrostates as a distinct category. This operates independently of general development level.

This structure suggests resource endowments and economic complexity operate as partially independent factors, challenging deterministic "resource curse" narratives.

3. Heterogeneity in Resource-Rich Countries

The five identified clusters demonstrate substantial heterogeneity among resource producers:

  • Successful Diversifiers: Norway, Canada combine high resource production with high complexity
  • Petrostates: Gulf countries maintain extreme resource dependence with varied complexity outcomes
  • Emerging Producers: Some countries show rising production without corresponding complexity gains
  • Development Pathways: No single trajectory for resource-rich countries—outcomes depend on complementary factors

Case Studies: Contrasting Pathways

Norway (Cluster 2 → Success)

  • Strategy: Sovereign wealth fund, human capital investment
  • ECI: Maintained high complexity (>1.5) despite oil wealth
  • Institutions: Strong rule of law, democratic accountability
  • Outcome: Avoided resource curse through institutional quality

Venezuela (Cluster 2 → Challenge)

  • Challenge: High oil dependence (>40% GDP in 2010s)
  • ECI: Declining complexity, narrowing export base
  • Institutions: Weak governance, corruption
  • Outcome: Economic crisis following commodity price collapse

Chile (Cluster 3 → Moderate Success)

  • Resources: Copper dominance (15-20% exports)
  • Diversification: Growing manufacturing, services sectors
  • ECI: Positive trajectory, rising complexity
  • Policy: Fiscal rules, education investment

Zambia (Cluster 3 → Struggling)

  • Resources: Heavy copper dependence (>70% exports)
  • Challenge: Limited diversification progress
  • ECI: Stagnant, low complexity
  • Constraints: Infrastructure gaps, human capital deficits

Econometric & Machine Learning Results

Econometric Insights

  • Resource Curse: When controlling for country and year fixed effects, resources do not inherently reduce complexity
  • Human Capital: HCI consistently positive and significant
  • Interaction Effect: HCI × Production term suggests returns to human capital amplified in resource-rich contexts
  • Institutions Matter: Rule of law and property rights show positive associations

Machine Learning Insights

  • Top Predictors: HCI, institutional quality, capital formation
  • Interaction Importance: HCI × Production ranks higher than traditional macro variables
  • Model Performance: Regularized models achieve strong predictive accuracy
  • Robustness: Key findings consistent across LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net

Policy Implications

Beyond Simple Narratives

Results challenge simplistic "resource curse" framing. Resource production does not deterministically reduce economic complexity. Policy focus should shift from whether countries have resources to how institutions and human capital shape resource management.

Context-Specific Strategies

The identified clusters suggest different strategic priorities:

  • High-resource, low-complexity: Invest in education and institutions
  • Moderate-resource: Balance extraction with manufacturing development
  • Low-resource: Focus on complexity growth independent of resources

Methodological Notes

Data Processing

  • Resource Aggregation: 20+ commodities grouped into 4 categories
  • Production Values: Physical quantity × market price (USD)
  • GDP Normalization: Production / (GDP per capita × Population)
  • Standardization: Mean=0, Std=1 before PCA/clustering

Statistical Methods

  • PCA: 2 components, 53.1% variance explained
  • K-means: k=5, silhouette score = 0.481
  • Panel Regression: Country and year fixed effects
  • ML: Cross-validated regularized regression

Limitations & Extensions

  • Production values use global prices, not country-specific rents or extraction costs
  • Clustering based on single year (2019); panel clustering could reveal temporal patterns
  • Causal identification requires instrumental variables or natural experiments
  • Future work: Deep learning for nonlinear complexity transitions, case studies of successful diversifiers

Appendix: Methodological Details

Principal Component Analysis

PCA is used to reduce dimensionality before clustering. The first two components explain 53.1% of total variance.

PC1 – Economic Development (29.4%)

  • High loadings on Economic Complexity Index and Human Capital Index
  • Separates advanced manufacturing economies from low-income countries
  • Weak negative relationship with metals production

PC2 – Hydrocarbon Production (23.7%)

  • Driven by oil and natural gas production
  • Identifies energy exporters
  • Negative association with coal production

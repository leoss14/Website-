Natural Resources & Economic Complexity

Econometric analysis of resource dependence and development pathways across 208 countries. Capstone project with Moody's Ratings.

Research Question

Do natural resource endowments constrain or enable economic diversification? How do resource-rich countries differ in their paths toward economic complexity?

The "resource curse" hypothesis suggests that countries rich in natural resources often experience slower economic growth and lower development outcomes compared to resource-poor countries. This project examines this relationship empirically across 208 countries, analyzing patterns of natural resource production alongside measures of economic complexity, human capital, and institutional quality.

Analytical Approach

The analysis integrates unsupervised learning (PCA and K-means clustering), econometric methods (panel regression with fixed effects), and machine learning (regularized regression) to identify patterns in how countries navigate resource wealth. Four resource categories are examined: hydrocarbons (oil, gas, coal), metals (iron, copper, gold, etc.), minerals (phosphate, potash), and agricultural commodities.

Key Findings

1. Resource Production is Highly Concentrated

Global natural resource production exhibits extreme concentration. A small number of countries account for the majority of production across all resource categories, while most countries show minimal resource intensity.

  • Top 3 producers dominate: China ($280.5B), USA ($273.2B), and Russia ($200.9B) account for 61.6% of production among the top 10 producers
  • High resource dependence is rare: Only 10 countries show resource production exceeding 7.5% of GDP (Kuwait 15.8%, Congo Rep. 15.1%, Qatar 12.5%)
  • Hydrocarbon concentration: USA, Saudi Arabia, and Russia dominate oil production ($176.5B, $121.9B, $120.3B respectively)
  • Metals concentration: China alone produces $90.7B in metals, followed by Australia ($25.6B) and Chile ($24.4B)

2. Two Principal Dimensions Structure Resource-Development Relationships

PCA reveals that variation in resource-economic patterns is primarily structured along two orthogonal dimensions:

PC1 - Economic Development (31.6%)

Countries vary primarily by economic sophistication and human capital, largely independent of resource endowments. High-complexity economies cluster together regardless of resource production.

PC2 - Hydrocarbon Intensity (24.1%)

A second dimension captures oil and gas production intensity, identifying petrostates as a distinct category. This operates independently of general development level.

This suggests that while economic development and resource wealth are related, they represent distinct sources of variation - economic complexity is not mechanically determined by resource abundance.

4. Human Capital Mediates Resource Effects

The relationship between resources and complexity is mediated by human capital investment. Countries with high human capital indices show positive or neutral relationships between resource production and economic complexity.

The HCI x Production interaction term is positive and significant, suggesting that returns to human capital are amplified in resource-rich contexts rather than diminished.

This finding challenges simplistic "resource curse" narratives and suggests that institutional and educational investments can transform resource wealth into a development asset.

Cluster Transitions (1995–2019)

Static cluster assignments mask an important question: how stable are these groupings over time? The Sankey diagram below traces country movements between clusters from 1995 to 2019, revealing which development pathways are common and which are rare.

Transition Patterns

Of 126 countries tracked across both years, 79% remained in their original cluster. The remaining 21% (26 countries) shifted categories. Several patterns stand out:

  • Upward mobility is possible but uncommon: 12 countries moved from "Low-Income Diversified" to "Advanced Economies" — including Brazil, Malaysia, Thailand, Türkiye, and the Philippines. These transitions typically accompanied sustained manufacturing development and human capital accumulation.
  • Mining intensification: 6 countries moved into "Mining-Dependent" status, including Peru, DRC, Ghana, and Bolivia — reflecting expanded metals extraction relative to GDP.
  • Petrostate persistence: 10 of 13 petrostates in 1995 remained petrostates in 2019. Norway and Qatar moved to "Wealthy Hydrocarbon Exporters," combining high oil/gas production with greater economic complexity.
  • No upward mobility from petrostates: No country transitioned directly from "Petrostates" to "Advanced Economies" during this period.

Econometric & Machine Learning Results

Econometric Insights

  • Resource Curse: When controlling for country and year fixed effects, resources do not inherently reduce complexity
  • Human Capital: HCI consistently positive and significant
  • Interaction Effect: HCI x Production term suggests returns to human capital amplified in resource-rich contexts
  • Institutions Matter: Rule of law and property rights show positive associations

Machine Learning Insights

  • Top Predictors: Human capital and rule of law dominate feature importance
  • Heterogeneous Effects: Resource curse varies by country type — oil is positive for diversified economies, negative for resource-dependent ones
  • Model Performance: Tree-based models (XGBoost, LightGBM) achieve R² ≈ 0.82 with interactions
  • Interaction Value: Adding high-resource country interactions improves test R² by 1.7 percentage points

Policy Implications

Context-Dependent Resource Curse

The resource curse is not universal. Oil production associates positively with complexity in diversified economies (Canada, Norway) but negatively in resource-dependent countries. Policy should focus on the conditions under which resources become constraints rather than treating resource wealth as inherently problematic.

Heterogeneous Strategies

The interaction analysis suggests differentiated approaches:

  • High-resource, low-complexity: Prioritize human capital and institutional reform
  • Diversified resource producers: Leverage resource revenues for complexity-enhancing investments
  • Metals-dependent economies: Focus on downstream processing and manufacturing linkages

Methodological Notes

Data Processing

  • Resource Aggregation: 20+ commodities grouped into 4 categories
  • Production Values: Physical quantity x market price (USD)
  • GDP Normalization: Production / (GDP per capita x Population)
  • Standardization: Mean=0, Std=1 before PCA/clustering

Statistical Methods

  • PCA: 2 components, 55.7% variance explained
  • K-means: k=6, silhouette score = 0.344
  • Panel Regression: Country and year fixed effects
  • ML: Temporal split (train ≤2013, test 2014-2019), ridge/tree models with interaction terms

Appendix: Methodological Details

Resource Diversity Decomposition

The diversity index measures each country's global market share across four resource categories: oil, natural gas, coal, and metals. Higher scores indicate greater dominance in global production. A power transformation (x^1.5) rewards concentration without extreme distortion. The chart below decomposes this index for the top 20 producing countries.

China dominates with a score of 4,123 — nearly five times higher than second-place Australia (850). This reflects China's outsized share of global metals and coal production. The DRC ranks third (571) due to its dominance in specific metals markets (cobalt, copper). Countries like Chile and Peru appear high due to concentrated metals extraction rather than diversified portfolios.

Principal Component Analysis

PCA is used to reduce dimensionality before clustering. The first two components explain 55.7% of total variance.

PC1 - Economic Development (31.6%)

  • High loadings on Economic Complexity Index (0.67) and Human Capital Index (0.62)
  • Separates advanced manufacturing economies from low-income countries
  • Negative relationship with metals production (-0.39)

PC2 - Hydrocarbon Production (24.1%)

  • Driven by oil (0.64) and natural gas (0.54) production
  • Identifies energy exporters
  • Negative association with coal production (-0.40)
